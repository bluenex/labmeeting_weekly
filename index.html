Weekly Journal Discussion

Virtual Reality for Sensorimotor Rehabilitation Post-Stroke: The Promise and Current State of the Field (2013)

Week 5

Introduction

  • It was suggested that VR could deliver training intensity (repetitions and duration) associated with neuroplasticity and positive behavioral adaptations
  • Early reviews of the field have shown that promise to be partially met
  • The study of virtual reality-enhanced rehabilitation has a short life of approximately a decade
  • There are two alternating bi-annual international conferences
    • International Conference on Virtual Reality Rehabilitation - http://virtual-rehab.org/2013/
    • International Conference on Disability Virtual Reality and Associated Technologies - www. icdvrat.reading.ac.uk/
    • Recently members of both groups have formed the International Society for Virtual Reality - www.isvr.org

Methods

    Paper search
  • Search terms "virtual reality" and "stroke" from March 2010 to 2013

    • Inclusion criteria
  • RCTs that compared standard of care to VR, or compared different delivery methods in VR

    • Exclusion criteria
  • CEBM level of evidence lower than 3
  • Had a mechanistic or validation focus or used off-the-shelf video games

Search results

  • The search yielded 50 articles
  • Ten of which met the inclusion criteria
  • Eight are upper extremity rehabilitation studies
    and two are gait treatment studies

Results

Discussion

The participants

  • Typically in the chronic phase (> 6 months) post-stroke
  • One study for acute (4-22 days post) and two for sub-acute (< 3 months)
  • Cognitive and perceptual characteristics of the participants
    were mentioned in all studies
    • Future characterization of the cognitive, sensory and perceptual abilities of the subjects participating in trials will increase both the external validity and targeted application of VR. It will also reduce the artificial separation between cognitive and motor rehabilitation.
  • Most participants had moderately severe motor control impairments
    (UEFMA 30-42 out of 44)
  • One study sample into moderately and mildly impaired groups, with more impaired subjects making larger magnitude gains with both VR and control interventions

The hardware

  • Robotic or motion sensing systems
  • Five of UL studies used a motion-sensing interface
  • Neither interface appeared to yield superior motor outcomes
  • Four UL systems were enhanced with haptics, three were purely visually based, and one had a mixed reality where subjects interacted with real world objects
The current literature does not point to robust clinically meaningful activity level outcomes for haptics added to VR systems.

The simulations

  • Were evenly grouped into two categories: replication of real world tasks such as reaching and walking, or game-based tasks, such as flying a plane
  • Reaching simulations consistently improved reaching abilities, while game-based UL interventions produced less specific outcomes
  • Task-specific training yielded superior results in the UL studies, but not in the gait studies
  • Most UL systems (6 of 8) were in 3D
However, while the fidelity of movement is enhanced with 3D immersive systems, they are not superior to 2D non-immersive systems in promoting real world activity level motor outcomes.

The frequency, duration and distribution

  • Training was typically distributed over a week
  • The average duration was approximately 4 weeks (UL and gait), but was 12 weeks for some UL studies
  • Total training time for the UL studies was an average of 10.5 h
  • UL studies supported a dose–response relationship between increased training dose and larger improvements in motor function
    • Some of the strongest evidence related to the effectiveness of non-technology-based rehabilitation interventions suggests that outcomes are related to the dose of the intervention
  • It is not clear if training doses in VR will compare to real world training doses

The progression

  • Was achieved with software algorithms and clinician-tester input
  • Five of eight UL studies used algorithm for progression
  • For UL studies which lacked of an algorithm, the progression was not well described
  • The most sophisticated algorithm used movement parameters such as smoothness of sub-movements to shape the motor behavior and progress the therapy
It appears that robotic interfaces have the advantage of using sophisticated algorithms for treatment customization and progression. Whether this benefit merits the cost is still not clear.

The outcomes

  • Several of the UL studies demonstrated statistically significant improvements at the body function level and/or activity level for both VR groups and dose-matched controls.
  • It is also interesting to note that the largest magnitude improvements were reported in the acute stage of recovery
  • Beyond this clear observation, patterns for dose–response or acuity levels impacting outcomes were not apparent

Conclusion

Remaining questions

While the quantity of RCT’s examining VR for stroke rehabilitation is growing, there remain unresolved questions about the technology, clinical characteristics, and practical concerns that will affect translation of VR rehabilitation into practice.
    Technology related
  • Is it necessary to utilize haptic interfaces?
  • Are semi-immersive 2D systems as efficacious as immersive systems in reducing impairments and promoting activity?

  • Do software-controlled algorithms provide greater speed and fidelity of exercise progression?
  • Are they superior to expert clinical decision-making?
  • Can they be combined with clinician-decision making?
    Participant selection and specificity of training
  • What are the minimal cognitive and perceptual requirements to use virtual environments for sensorimotor rehabilitation effectively?
  • How will differences in motor severity, chronicity, and type of task interact with VR system capabilities and dose requirements?

    • Practical considerations
  • Will the cost of the sophisticated technology (such a robots interfaced with the VE’s) justify the benefits?

Suggestions

    Should be addressed in future study
  • The efficacy of VR interventions relative to the current standard of care
  • Comparing the efficacy and effectiveness of various approaches to presenting and delivering virtual interventions
