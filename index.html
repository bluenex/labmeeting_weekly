Week 2

Introduction

Cognition

  • The process of knowing
  • The discrimination between and
    selection of relevent information
  • The acquisition of information
  • The understanding and retention
  • The expression and application of
    knowledge in the appropriate situation

Impairments of cognitive function

  • Significant cause of disability after traumatic brain injury (TBI)
  • Often the most persistent of brain injury in patients with
    a good neurologic recovery
  • Interventions designed to recover cognitive function are
    a standard component of brain injury rehabilitation

Approaches of interventions

  1. Reinforcing, strengthening or reestablishing previously learned of behavior
  2. Establishing new patterns of cognitive activity through compensatory cognitive mechanisms
  3. Establishing new patterns of cognitive activity through external compensatory mechanisms (e.g. environmental structuring and support)
  4. Enabling persons to adapt to their cognitive disability in order to improve their overall level of functioning and quality of life


Regardless of the specific approach or area od intervention, cognitive rehabilitation should be directed at achieveing changes that improve individual function in areas that are relevent to their everyday lives

Cognitive rehabilitation studies

Treatment efficacy
studies
  • Highly constrained studies
  • Time-limited interventions
  • Selected, homogenous samples
  • Research purposes
  • Less useful for initially establishing the effectiveness
Studies of clinical effectiveness
  • Almost impossible to get the desired conditions of samples

Method

Literature Filtering

  • Search words:
    • attention, awareness, cognition, communication, executive, language, memory, perception, problem solving, reasoning, rehabilitation, remediation, and training
  • 655 published articles
  • Exclusion criteria:
    1. reports not addressing intervention
    2. theoretical articles or descriptions of treatment approaches
    3. review papers
    4. reports without adequate specification of interventions
    5. subjects other than persons with TBI or stroke (8 reports were retained that included diagnoses of “other brain injury” when these clearly represented a minority of subjects or when it was possible to distinguish the results for the subjects with TBI and stroke)
    6. pediatric subjects
    7. single case reports without empirical data
    8. non–peer-reviewed articles and book chapters
    9. pharmacologic interventions
    10. non-English language papers
  • Finally, 171 studies remaining for the review
